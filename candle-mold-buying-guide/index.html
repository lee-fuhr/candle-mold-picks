How to choose candle molds

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Focus on mold material (thick, food-grade silicone), shape (pillar, taper, or geometric), size (diameter and height for your desired candle), and ease of release (smooth interior, proper wick holes). Read owner feedback to ensure the mold holds up to repeated use with your chosen wax.

What type of silicone is best for candle molds?

The quality of silicone determines how long your mold lasts and how clean your candles turn out. Look for food-grade or platinum-cure silicone, it’s more heat-resistant and won’t leach odors or oils into your wax. Thick walls (several millimeters) help the mold hold its shape when pouring hot wax and resist tearing during demolding. Some molds are made with lower-grade silicone that feels tacky or overly flexible; these can deform after a few uses and leave rough surfaces on your candles. Reputable makers use a firm but pliable silicone that balances durability with easy release. Avoid bargain molds that feel thin or have a strong chemical smell out of the package.

How do pillar, taper, and geometric molds differ?

Pillar molds are straight-sided cylinders, usually with a flat bottom, and they produce classic column candles. Taper molds taper inward from base to top, creating the iconic dinner-candle shape that requires careful wick centering and a narrow wick hole. Geometric molds include cubes, hexagons, stars, or faceted shapes that can be tricky to demold cleanly because of sharp corners and undercuts. Each shape affects wicking and cooling. Pillars allow easy wick placement, while tapers often need a special wick tab or pin to hold the wick centered. Geometric molds demand a flexible silicone that won’t trap air bubbles in tight angles. Many candle makers start with pillars for practice before moving to tapers or complex geometries.

What size and dimensions should I look for?

Mold diameter and height directly control burn time and stability. A wide pillar (several inches across) burns slowly and works well for centerpiece candles, while a narrow taper (around an inch wide at the base) burns faster and needs a snug holder. Height also affects how the candle pools, taller molds can produce a longer but narrower flame if not wicked correctly. Volume is important too. One-piece silicone molds often list capacity in fluid ounces or grams of wax, but those numbers can vary with your wax density. A good rule: check owner reviews to see how much wax people actually use for that specific mold. For geometric shapes, consider the widest point to ensure it fits your pouring pot and curing space.

How important is mold release and ease of use?

A well-made silicone mold should release candles without greasing or spraying. The interior should be glossy-smooth, any texture will transfer to the candle. Many quality molds have a small slit or split along one side to help peel the silicone away from the wax without distorting the shape. Taper molds often come with a removable base or a wick hole that aligns with a centering pin. Some molds need a tiny amount of mold release spray for intricate details, but if you’re constantly fighting to get the candle out, the mold design is likely flawed. Also check that the wick hole is large enough for your wick size and easy to thread, a too-tight hole will snap brittle wicks during assembly.

What waxes work best with silicone molds?

Paraffin wax is the most forgiving for silicone molds because it shrinks noticeably upon cooling, making release easier. Soy wax has less shrinkage and can stick more, so a flexible silicone mold and gentle demolding are essential. Beeswax is tacky and requires a very smooth silicone surface, plus its higher melting point demands that the silicone be rated for at least 180°F (check the manufacturer’s heat tolerance). Pillar blends (containing additives like stearic acid) are ideal for tall straight molds because they hold shape and release cleanly. For geometric molds, softer waxes like container wax can fill details better but may be harder to unmold without breaking sharp edges. Always test a small batch before committing to a full pour.

Are there any common pitfalls to avoid?

Beware of dirt-cheap silicone molds that are too thin or have a poor finish, they often tear after one or two uses, and rough interiors leave cloudy or cracked candles. Another trap is buying a taper mold that doesn’t have a proper wick pin or centering tab; without it, your wick will drift and produce crooked flames. Also avoid molds with sharp inner seams where the silicone was joined, those create flash lines on the candle that are hard to trim. And never pour wax above the silicone’s recommended temperature; even food-grade silicone can degrade if you exceed its limit. Quality molds cost a bit more upfront but easily pay for themselves over dozens of pours.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use any silicone mold for candles?

No. Only use molds labeled for candle-making or high-temperature food-grade silicone. Baking molds may not withstand repeated hot wax pours, and craft-store molds can contain fillers that melt or warp.

How do I calculate how much wax I need for a mold?

Fill the mold with water, then pour that water into a measuring cup to get the volume in fluid ounces. Multiply by 0.85 to roughly estimate ounces of wax (since wax is lighter than water). For accuracy, check owner reviews for that specific mold’s typical wax load.

Do I need to use mold release spray?

Not for most quality silicone molds if your wax is compatible. But if you’re using natural wax like beeswax or an intricately shaped geometric mold, a light spritz of mold release can prevent sticking and protect fine details.

How do I clean silicone candle molds?

Wash with warm soapy water and a soft sponge after each use. Avoid abrasive scrubbers that scratch the silicone. For stuck wax residue, freeze the mold for 15 minutes, then flex it to pop the wax off, do not use metal tools.

What’s the difference between a taper and a pillar mold?

A taper mold makes candles that are narrow at the base and widen toward the top, requiring a centering pin for the wick. A pillar mold produces straight-sided cylinders with a flat bottom, easier to wick and typically more stable during burning.

Can I reuse silicone molds many times?

Yes. With proper care, a thick food-grade silicone mold can last for dozens or even hundreds of uses. Inspect after each pour for tears or clouding, and store flat or rolled loosely (never folded) to preserve the silicone’s shape.

In shortPick a mold with thick, food-grade silicone, a smooth interior, and well-designed wick holes. Match the shape and size to your candle style and wax choice, and rely on real owner experiences to confirm the mold holds up over time. A thoughtful purchase now means clean pours and long-lasting use.
